## Grant Voice Profile

### Tone Calibration

**Grant mode**: 30% understated enthusiasm / 70% confident precision

Grant writing is the most formal register — closer to manuscript voice but with a persuasive edge. You are making a case for funding, not reporting findings.

| Core Voice Attribute | X Article Expression | Grant Expression |
|---------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Rigorous enthusiasm | "Mind-blowing" / "Absolutely wild" | "This approach will reveal..." / "Our preliminary data demonstrate..." |
| First-person stakes | "As a [your role] in [your city]..." | "As a [your role], I bridge [domain A] with [domain B]..." |
| Intellectual humility | "And... I was wrong." | "Should [alternative outcome], we will [contingency plan]..." |
| The Big BUT | "BUT -- and it's a big BUT" | "However, a critical barrier to progress is..." |
| Cross-domain linking | Explicit bridge paragraphs | Integrated in Significance (why now) and Innovation (what's novel) |

### Confidence Level

- **USE:** "We will determine..." / "Our preliminary data demonstrate..." / "This approach will reveal..."
- **AVOID uncertainty signals:** "We hope to..." / "We might find..." / "It is possible that..."
- **ALSO AVOID overstatement:** "We will definitively prove..." / "This will revolutionize..."

### Register

Formal but accessible — write as if explaining your work to a smart colleague in an adjacent field. Aim for sentences under 20 words on average. One idea per paragraph with a clear topic sentence.

- **Specific Aims and Significance**: Narrative — a story with beginning (problem), middle (approach), and end (impact)
- **Approach section**: More telegraphic — detailed, methodical, almost protocol-like

### Voice Rules

**DO:**
- Use **active voice** and **first person** ("we" for R01; "I" for K/F where you are the trainee)
- Lead with clinical urgency, then pivot to molecular specificity
- Provide a **testable hypothesis** based on preliminary data
- Use **bold text** for key structural terms (gap, hypothesis, aim titles, expected outcomes) — scannability is respect for reviewer time
- Write for a **PhD-level scientist outside your subfield**
- Use **explicit signposting**: "The central hypothesis of this proposal is..." / "The rationale for the proposed research is..."
- Include **contingency plans** for every aim — "Should [X] fail, we will..."
- Make aims **independently completable** — no "domino aims"
- Connect every aim to the **translational arc**
- Use **parallel grammatical structure** across aims

**DO NOT:**
- Use exclamatory markers without evidence
- Use hedging: "We hope to..." / "We believe that perhaps..."
- Use "It is well known that..." — wastes space; just cite
- Use "This is the first study to..." — often untrue; use "This approach uniquely integrates..."
- Use "In order to" (verbose), "utilizing" (pompous), "It should be noted that..." (throat-clearing)
- Include citations on the Specific Aims page (it should stand alone)
- Use excessive abbreviations without definition

### Key Rhetorical Strategies

**Problem/Gap/Hook Framework**: Open by establishing a real-world problem → identify a specific gap in knowledge → hook the reader with why filling this gap matters.

**Language Patterns to Adopt:**

| Purpose | Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Stating the gap | "However, [unknown] represents a critical barrier to [goal]." | "However, the functional consequences of psychiatric risk variants in specific cell types remain unknown, representing a critical barrier to developing targeted therapeutics." |
| Stating the hypothesis | "Our central hypothesis is that [X], based on [preliminary evidence]." | — |
| Stating innovation | "This proposal is innovative because it [novelty], departing from [status quo] by [how]." | — |
| Impact statement | "Upon completion, this work will [outcome], which will [broader impact]." | — |

**Phrases to Avoid:**

| Avoid | Why | Instead |
|-------|-----|---------|
| "We hope to..." | Signals uncertainty | "We will determine..." |
| "It is well known that..." | Wastes space | Just state the fact with a citation |
| "This is the first study to..." | Often untrue | "This approach uniquely integrates..." |
| "Paradigm-shifting" (standalone) | Overused | Describe the specific shift |
| "Utilizing" | Pompous | "Using" |
| "In order to" | Verbose | "To" |

### Grant Mechanism-Specific Voice

| Feature | F31/F32 (Fellowship) | K99/R00 (Pathway) | K01/K08 (Career Dev) | R01 (Research) |
|---------|---------------------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| **Voice** | "I will learn X to do Y" | "I am becoming independent" | "I have A, B; I need C, D" | "We will determine X" |
| **Key move** | "I am uniquely positioned because..." | "My mentored phase equips me to launch..." | "Bridging X with new skills in Y enables..." | "We are positioned to answer this because..." |
| **Biggest pitfall** | Not distinguishing from mentor | Not showing independence trajectory | Not articulating skill gaps | Overly ambitious scope |

### Author-Specific Grant Patterns

**Identity Statement** (use across applications):
> [YOUR IDENTITY STATEMENT: Replace with a 2-3 sentence summary of your training and expertise. Example: "I graduated from [program] at [institution] with a PhD in [field]. I gained extensive experience in [methods/domains] and developed [key contributions]."]

This should convey your unique positioning and expertise.

**Clinical Analogy as Hook** (a signature opener):
> "While cardiologists have blood tests to help diagnose heart attacks and oncologists have PET scans to find hidden cancers, psychiatrists don't have objective tests that solidify a diagnosis of schizophrenia."

This "other fields have X, psychiatry doesn't" pattern is effective and should be preserved.

**Research Program Narrative**: [YOUR ARC: e.g., early training → methodological expertise → current focus → future direction]. Each application positions the proposed work as a natural extension of this arc.

**Team/Synergy Framing**:
> [YOUR TEAM FRAMING: e.g., "The synergy between my skillset in [your methods] and Dr. [Mentor]'s experience with [their expertise] will allow me to effectively carry out the specific aims."]

### NIH-Specific Requirements

**Simplified Review Framework (January 2025+)**:
- **Importance of the Research** — Should this research be done? Why? (1-9 scale)
- **Rigor and Feasibility** — Can it be done well? (1-9 scale)
- **Expertise and Resources** — Appropriate / Needs additional (pass/fail)

Factor 3 is now pass/fail — strategic advantage for early-career applicants. Emphasize Factors 1 and 2.

**Rigor and Reproducibility (required)**: Scientific premise, rigorous experimental design, biological variables (sex as biological variable must be addressed), authentication of key resources.

**Formatting**: 11pt or larger (Arial, Georgia, Helvetica, Palatino Linotype). 0.5 inch margins minimum. Page limits strictly enforced — exceeding = instant rejection.

**NIMH Strategic Alignment** for your work: "variant-to-function mapping," "cell-type-specific mechanisms," "translational pipeline," "[your applied domain]."

**Key Evidence**: Pier et al. (2018) PNAS showed inter-rater reliability among 43 reviewers evaluating 25 R01s was essentially zero (ICC = 0). Implications: Clarity and accessibility are paramount. First impressions matter disproportionately. Submit persistently — volume correlates with success.

## Templates

### Template 1: Specific Aims Page (1 page)

```
SPECIFIC AIMS

[PARAGRAPH 1: THE OPENING — Problem + Gap + Hook]
[Sentence 1 — Hook: Compelling statement about disease/problem with urgency.]
[Sentences 2-4 — Context: Establish what is known. Use hard numbers.]
[Sentences 5-6 — Gap: "However, a critical barrier to progress is..." or "What remains unknown is..."]
[Final sentence — Fill: Your proposal addresses this gap.]

[PARAGRAPH 2: WHAT WE WILL DO]
The long-term goal of [my/our] research is [broad goal beyond this grant].
The objective of this application is [specific to this grant], guided by our
central hypothesis that [testable hypothesis], based on [preliminary evidence].
The rationale for the proposed research is that [what becomes possible if
hypothesis is supported].

[PARAGRAPH 3: THE AIMS]
To test this hypothesis, we propose the following aims:

**Aim 1: [Action verb] [what] [how].**
[1-2 sentences with working hypothesis.]

**Aim 2: [Action verb] [what] [how].**
[1-2 sentences with working hypothesis.]

**Aim 3 (if R01): [Action verb] [what] [how].**
[Optional for K/F mechanisms.]

[PARAGRAPH 4: THE PAYOFF]
[Expected outcomes and their significance.]
[How results advance the field regardless of whether hypothesis is confirmed.]
[Connection to long-term translational impact.]
```

**Quality Checks:**
- [ ] Fits on exactly one page (11pt, 0.5" margins)
- [ ] No citations (page should stand alone)
- [ ] Central hypothesis is explicit and testable
- [ ] Aims are independent (no domino aims)
- [ ] Key terms are bolded (gap, hypothesis, aim titles)
- [ ] A PhD outside the subfield can follow the argument
- [ ] Translational impact is stated concretely

### Template 2: Research Strategy — Significance (1-2 pages for R01)

```
SIGNIFICANCE

[PARAGRAPH 1: THE PROBLEM]
[Disease burden, prevalence, mortality/morbidity. Use hard numbers.
Current treatment limitations. Why should anyone care?]

[PARAGRAPH 2: STATE OF THE FIELD]
[What is known, with emphasis on recent advances that create
the opportunity for your work. Brief — not a literature review.]

[PARAGRAPH 3: THE GAP]
[What remains unknown. Be specific — not "more research is needed"
but "the functional impact of non-coding risk variants in specific
neural cell types remains uncharacterized."]

[PARAGRAPH 4: WHY THIS MATTERS NOW]
[Why the gap is addressable now: new technologies, new datasets,
preliminary data. Make the reader feel that NOT funding this
would be a missed opportunity.]

[PARAGRAPH 5: WHAT CHANGES IF SUCCESSFUL]
[Concrete advance in knowledge and/or clinical practice.]
```

## Workflow (Phases 0–6)

### Phase 0: Setup

1. **Read voice profiles** above
2. **Create working folder**: `$CWD/YYMMDD_Brief_Name/`
   - Example: `$CWD/260309_K08_Precision_Psychiatry/`
3. **Scan context folder** for relevant materials (RFA, prior applications, mentor biosketches)
4. **Determine entry point from arguments**:
   - Empty → Phase 1 (Interview)
   - Grant name/mechanism → Phase 2 (RFA Analysis)
   - `"specific-aims"` → Phase 3 directly
   - `"resume"` → Find most recent drafts/grant/ folder

### Phase 1: Interview

**Understand the application:**
- What mechanism? (F31, F32, K08, K99/R00, R01, CSTP, other)
- What funding agency? (NIH/NIMH, foundation, institutional)
- What is the deadline?
- Do you have the RFA/FOA number or URL?
- Is this a resubmission? If so, do you have the summary statement?

**Understand the research:**
- What's the central research question? (one sentence)
- What's the central hypothesis?
- What preliminary data do you have?
- Who are your mentors/collaborators?
- What's the long-term goal of your research program?

**Understand the context:**
- Who is likely to review this? (study section name if known)
- Have you talked to the program officer?
- Are there any reviewer concerns from prior submissions to address?

### Phase 2: RFA/FOA Analysis

**If the user provides an RFA/FOA:**
1. Fetch the RFA using WebFetch if a URL is provided
2. Extract: eligible mechanisms, page limits, review criteria, special emphasis areas, budget caps, LOI requirements
3. Identify alignment between your research and RFA priorities
4. Flag any misalignment

**If no specific RFA:**
- Use the standard NIH parent announcement for the mechanism
- Check NIMH strategic priorities for alignment language

**Deep Research (if needed):**
- Use `deep-researcher` agent to investigate: what makes high-scoring grants for this mechanism, recently funded grants in this area, study section composition

### Phase 3: Specific Aims

The Specific Aims page is the single most important page of any application.

Draft following Template 1 above:
1. **Opening paragraph**: Problem → Gap → Hook → Fill — Lead with urgency. Use the analogy pattern: "While [field A] has X, [field B] lacks Y"
2. **What We Will Do paragraph**: Long-term goal → Objective → Hypothesis → Rationale
3. **The Aims**: 2-3 independently completable aims. Use parallel grammatical structure. Aims must NOT depend on each other.
4. **Payoff paragraph**: Expected outcomes → Field advancement → Translational impact

Apply quality checklist above. Present to the user for approval before proceeding.

### Phase 4: Research Strategy

1. **Draft Significance first** — make the case for WHY
   - Use NIMH strategic priority language where aligned
2. **Draft Innovation** — make the case for WHAT'S NEW
   - 2-3 dimensions: conceptual, methodological, technological/translational
3. **Draft Approach** — make the case for HOW
   - Start with Preliminary Data (1-2 pages with publication-quality figures)
   - For each aim: Rationale → Experimental Design → Analysis Plan → Expected Outcomes → Pitfalls/Alternatives → Timeline
   - Power analyses for all statistical tests
4. **Add Rigor and Reproducibility section**
   - Scientific premise, experimental design rigor, sex as a biological variable, authentication of key resources

**Quality checks:**
- [ ] Every claim has supporting evidence or preliminary data
- [ ] Power analyses included for key experiments
- [ ] Contingency plans for every aim
- [ ] Rigor and reproducibility addressed
- [ ] SABV addressed (or justified)
- [ ] Within page limits
- [ ] Key terms defined for non-specialist reviewers

**Save:**
- `$CWD/YYMMDD_Name/specific_aims.md`
- `$CWD/YYMMDD_Name/research_strategy.md`

### Phase 5: Supporting Sections

**For K/F mechanisms:**
1. **Candidate Statement** — Follow Template 5. Use the established arc from exemplars above.
2. **Training Plan** — Follow Template 6. Specific, measurable goals with named courses, workshops, conferences.

**For all mechanisms:**
- **Data Management and Sharing Plan** (required since January 2023)
- **Biosketches** — remind the user to update for all key personnel

**Save:**
- `$CWD/YYMMDD_Name/candidate.md`
- `$CWD/YYMMDD_Name/training_plan.md` (K/F only)

### Phase 6: Review & Iterate

Ask the user:
- "Does the Specific Aims page hook you in the first sentence?"
- "Is the central hypothesis clear and testable?"
- "Are the aims truly independent?"
- "Is the scope realistic for the award period?"
- "Have you gotten feedback from your mentor(s)?"

**Pre-submission checklist:**
- [ ] All sections within page limits
- [ ] Formatting compliant (font, margins)
- [ ] All biosketches current
- [ ] Budget aligns with proposed work
- [ ] Reporting checklist completed (if required)
- [ ] Data management plan included
- [ ] Letters of support obtained
- [ ] Mock review feedback addressed

## Key Reminders

- **Never fabricate citations or data** — flag as `[VERIFY]` or `[DATA]` if unsure
- **Grant voice ≠ manuscript voice** — confident, persuasive, accessible
- **One grant = one central hypothesis** — everything serves the testable question
- **The Specific Aims page is everything** — if it doesn't compel, nothing else matters
- **Reviewers are not specialists in your exact area** — write for a PhD in an adjacent field
- **Submit persistently** — Pier et al. (2018) showed near-zero reviewer agreement; volume correlates with success
- **Commit when finalized**: `draft: {Mechanism} {Title} ($CWD/YYMMDD_Name/)`
